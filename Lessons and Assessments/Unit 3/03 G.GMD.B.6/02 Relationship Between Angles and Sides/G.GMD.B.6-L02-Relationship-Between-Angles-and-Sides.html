Opposite Sides

This lesson investigates how each angle measurement of a triangle affects the side across or opposite from that angle. In the diagram below in \(\triangle ABC\), \(\angle A\) is always opposite the side created with the other two vertices of B and C to create side \(\overline{BC}\). The same is true for the other two vertices and their opposite sides.

The Unequal Sides Theorem

The Unequal Sides Theorem states that when angles are of different measure in a triangle, the angle opposite from the longest side is greater than the angle opposite from the shortest side. This means the largest angle is always across from the longest side and the smallest angle is always across from the shortest side. Examine the following diagram to understand the relationship.

The converse of the Unequal Sides Theorem is also true. The largest angle must be opposite from the longest side and the smallest angle must be opposite from the shortest side.

The following examples will instruct how to list the sides from least to greatest using the theorem.

2. List Angles from Least to Greatest
Instruction Examples Interactive Video Self-Check

The reverse of the Unequal Sides Theorem is also true. This states that the side opposite from the largest angle is longer than the side opposite from the smallest angle. This means the longest side is always across from the largest angle and the shortest side is always across from the smallest angle. The same diagram applies as before.

Listing angles from least to greatest can be confusing. In the examples tab, pay close attention on how to list them.

Solution:

First, color code the angles and sides to help pair up each side with its opposite angle. In order to list the angles from least to greatest, first list the sides from least to greatest.

Now, by using the opposite angles in relation to the sides, \(\angle A\) is the smallest angle and \(\angle I\) is the largest. Be careful when writing the inequality, the \(m\) symbol to represent the measurement must be written for each angle, along with the angle symbol and the less than symbol. Use less than symbols since the previous angle must be less than then next. Altogether from least to greatest, the angles are: \(m\angle A \lt m\angle R\lt m\angle I\)

3. Apply Algebra to the Unequal Sides Theorem
Instruction Examples Video Self-Check

The Unequal Sides Theorem can also be used when only one or even no angle measures and side lengths are known. By using algebra, writing sides in order from least to greatest can still be accomplished by using the Triangle Angle Sum Theorem as before.

Some questions will include converting written expressions into algebraic form. To begin, review the following common expressions.

Twice: two times another

Triple: three times another

Quadruple: four times another

Quintuple: five times another

Greater than: add the value to another number

Less than: subtract the value to another number

The following are converted from written expressions to algebraic expressions. \(x\) will be used as the other number.

  1. Triple a number: \(3x\)
  2. Eight more than twice a number: \(2x+8\)
  3. Seventeen less than a number: \(x-17\)

In the last two examples, the numbers added or subtracted are written after the unknown number because they are in addition to or subtracted from another. Remember this important attribute when creating equations in the following examples.

Example 3.1

Write Side Lengths in Order Using a Given Diagram

List the side lengths from least to greatest in \(\triangle ARG\). Write the answer as an inequality. Triangle is not drawn to scale.

Solution:

Use the Unequal Sides Theorem to list the sides in order. First find all missing angles by using the Triangle Angle Sum Theorem. Then list opposites sides from their angles in order.

Last, substitute the measures of the angles into your diagram to find the opposite sides in order from least to greatest.

The inequality for the length of the sides from least to greatest is \(CB \lt AC\lt AB\).

Example 3.2

Write Side Lengths in Order Without a Diagram Given

List the sides from least to greatest in \(\triangle QRS\). Write the answer as an inequality. Use the following information about each angle:

  • \(m\angle Q\) is fifty more than twice the \(m\angle R\).
  • \(m\angle S\) is ten less than quadruple the \(m\angle R\).

Solution:

Step One

First, draw a diagram for \(\triangle QRS\). It should be large enough so that you can write in the expressions for each angle. Since you do not know what type of triangle will be pictured, make any type of triangle you would like to use. Color coding the sides and opposite angles at the beginning will aid in ordering as well.

Step Two

After you have a general diagram drawn, create the expressions for each angle. There is information for \(m\angle Q\) and \(m\angle S\) given but no information for \(m\angle R\). Due to this, use a variable such as x to represent \(m\angle R\).

Now using the given information, write expressions for \(m\angle Q\) and \(m\angle S\) based on \(m\angle R\). For \(m\angle S\), make sure the ten is subtracted from the \(4x\) since it is ten less than the rest of the information.

Step Three

The steps are now the same from Example 3.1. Use the Angle Sum Theorem to find the value of \(x\). Substitute the answer into each expression to find the measure of each angle.

Step Four

Last, substitute the measures of the angles into your diagram to find the opposite sides in order from least to greatest.

The inequality for the length of the sides from least to greatest is \(SQ\lt RQ\lt RS\).
