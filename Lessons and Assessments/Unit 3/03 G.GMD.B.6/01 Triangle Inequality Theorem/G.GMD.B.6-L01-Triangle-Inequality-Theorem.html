Triangle Inequality Theorem

The Triangle Inequality Theorem states that the sum of any two sides of a triangle must be greater than the third side.

\(side\ 1+side\ 2>side\ 3\) or \(BC+AB>AC\)

\(side\ 2+side\ 3>side\ 1\) or \(AB+AC>BC\)

\(side\ 1+side\ 3>side\ 2\) or \(BC+AC>AB\)

A generalization and shortcut that can be made is as follows:

  • first short side + second short side > long side

Notice that the sum of the two shortest side lengths must be greater than the largest side. The sum can NOT be equal to the largest side because that would create segments that can not create any angle to make a triangle. This is because it will only create two line segments.

Lengths That Create a Triangle

Can the side lengths 6 cm, 7 cm, and 8 cm create a triangle?

Solution:

Using the method from the Instruction tab, any of the given side lengths can be used for sides 1, 2, or 3. The following will use 6 cm, 7 cm, and 8 cm as side 1, side 2, and side 3 respectively.

\(side\ 1+side\ 2>side\ 3\)

\(13>8\) ? True

\(side\ 2+side\ 3>side\ 1\)

\(7+8>6\) ?

\(15>6\) ? True

\(side\ 1+side\ 3>side\ 2\)

\(6+8>7\) ?

\(14>7\) ? True

Using the generalization, you only need to check that the sum of the two short sides is greater than that third side.

first short side + second short side > long side

\(6+7>8\) ?

\(13>8\) ? True

Since this case is true, it is possible to have a triangle with the side lengths of 6 cm, 7 cm, and 8 cm.

Example 1.2

Lengths That Do Not Create a Triangle

Can the side lengths 10 in, 7 in, and 3 in create a triangle?

Solution:

Using the method from the Instruction tab, any of the given side lengths can be used for sides 1, 2, or 3. The following will use 10 in, 7 in, and 3 in as side 1, side 2, and side 3 respectively.

\(side\ 1+side\ 2>side\ 3\)

\(10+7>3\) ?

\(17>3\) ? True

\(side\ 2+side\ 3>side\ 1\)

\(7+3>10\) ?

\(10>10\) ? False

Since you have found a false statement, you do not have to continue to the last inequality because you have found that the lengths 3 in, 7 in, and 10 in do not create a triangle.

Notice here that using the generalization would have given you the answer quicker by choosing to check the sum of the two shorter sides at the beginning.

\(side\ 2+side\ 3>side\ 1\)

\(7+3>10\) ?

\(10>10\) ? False

Example 1.3

Lengths That Do Not Create a Triangle

Can the side lengths 4.7 ft, 12.3 ft, and 4.7 ft create a triangle?

Solution:

Here, use the generalization to check if a triangle can be created. Notice that both short sides are congruent so they can be used interchangeably.

first short side + second short side > long side

\(4.7+4.7>12.3\) ?

\(9.4>12.3\) ? False

Since there is a false circumstance, side lengths of 4.7 ft, 12.3 ft, and 4.7 ft can not make a triangle.

2. Finding the Range of the Third Side
Instruction Examples Video Self-Check

Constructing Triangles When Only Two Side Lengths Are Given

Next you will apply the Triangle Inequality Theorem in order to find the possible side lengths of a triangle when only two side lengths are provided. Unfortunately, you will not know whether the given sides are the shortest, longest or a mix, so all scenarios will have to be assumed true in order to find a range of possible values for the third side.

Examine the examples to learn how to create a compound inequality in order to find all possible values of the third side of the triangle.

Example 2.1

Create a Range of Values for the Third Side of a Triangle

Find the range of possible values for \(\overline{AB}\) in \(\triangle ABC\). The triangle is not drawn to scale.

For questions like this, you do not know if you are given the two shortest, the two longest sides or one of each, so all situations must be investigated.

The generalization of the Triangle Inequality Theorem states that the sum of the two short sides must be greater than the longest side. Therefore, the length of the longest possible side must be less than the sum of the other two shorter sides. If \(\overline{AB}\) is the longest, then it must be less than 17 units long.

Also by the Triangle Inequality Theorem, if \(\overline{AB}\) is instead the shortest side of the triangle, find its value by subtracting the other short side to both sides of the equation in order isolate it.

In case this value is negative, the absolute value of the answer must be used because lengths are always positive.

To find the range of the third side you can solve two separate inequalities then combine them into one compound inequality. Always write the values least to greatest with the missing side in the middle and use less than symbols with the missing side in between. Now combine both inequalities into one statement to create a compound inequality. \(3\lt AB\lt 17\) This states that the third side can be greater than 3 units but less than 17 units long. Notice that 3 and 17 can not be used as values.
