Corresponding
×
Points, sides, edges, or angles in two or more figures that are images of each other with respect to a sequence of transformations. If two figures are congruent, their corresponding parts are congruent to each other.

Alternate Interior Angles
×

When you have two lines crossed by a transversal, Alternate Interior Angles (AIA) are angles on the inner side of two lines, but on opposite sides of the transversal.

When the lines are parallel, then the alternate interior angles are congruent.

Close
Alternate Exterior Angles
×

When you have two lines crossed by a transversal, Alternate Exterior Angles (AEA) are angles on the outer side of the two lines, but on opposite sides of the transversal.

When the lines are parallel, then the alternate exterior angles are congruent.

Close
Same-Side Interior Angles
×

When you have two lines crossed by a transversal, Same-Side Interior Angles (SSIA) are angles on the inner side of the two lines, and on the same side of the transversal.

When the lines are parallel, then the sum of the Same-Side Interior Angles equals \(180^{\circ}\).

Reflexive Property
×

Reflexive Property of Congruence is when two figures share the same side length, then that side length is congruent in both triangles.

In Unit 3, you will apply theorems of parallel lines and angles to find the measure of missing angles in triangles. The following will demonstrate how to apply one property into a triangle.

Decide which angles are congruent by the Alternate Interior Angles Theorem.

Here, the parallel lines dictate which angles must be congruent. To find the congruent alternate interior angles easier, redraw only the parallel lines and the transversal that is shared between both triangles as shown next:

The parallel lines are \(\overleftrightarrow {AB}\) and \(\overleftrightarrow {CD}\). The transversal is \(\overleftrightarrow {BC}\). By only drawing these three lines, it is easier to see that the alternate interior angles are \(\angle B\) and \(\angle C\).

Finally, since \(\overleftrightarrow {AB}\parallel \overleftrightarrow {CD}\), \(\angle B\cong \angle C\) by the Alternate Interior Angles Theorem.

Given: \(\overline{BD}\perp \overline{EC}\)

Solution:

Here, use the given information to try to find any sides or angles that would be congruent between the two triangles.

Since \(\overline{BD}\perp \overline{EC}\), both \(\angle BCE\) and \(\angle DCE\) are right angles.

Since the measure of every right angle equals \(90^{\circ }\), \(\angle BCE\cong \angle DCE\) by the definition of right angles.

Next, examine the triangles to see if there are any parallel lines or vertical angles. Neither of these are used here. Finally, investigate if there are any sides of the two triangles that overlap on top of eachother. Since the entire length of side \(\overline{CD}\) is used in both triangles, \(\overline{CD}\cong \overline{CD}\) by the Reflexive Property of Congruence.
