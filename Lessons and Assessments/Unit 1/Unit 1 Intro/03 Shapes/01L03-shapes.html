Congruence

Figures are congruent when all of their matching or corresponding measurements are equal. The symbol for congruent is \(\cong\). Examine the congruent segments and congruent angles below and note the labeling. Below, examine the types of figures that are often congruent.

Congruent Segments vs. Congruent Angles

Congruent Segments

Congruent segments use the same amount of dashes to denote congruence. Here, since the length of \(\overline{ED}\) equals the length of \(\overline{FG}\) (written \(ED=FG\)), then \(\overline{ED}\) must be congruent to \(\overline{FG}\), (written \(\overline{ED}\cong \overline{FG}\)).
Note: With an equal sign, the line segment is NOT used over the segmentsâ€™ letters, but with a congruent symbol, the line segment symbol is used because congruence compares figures and not the numbers. For example: \(ED = FG\), but \(\overline {ED}\cong\overline {FE}\).

Congruent Angles

Congruent angles use the same amount of curves or arcs to denote congruence. Here, since the measure of \(\angle LVM\) equals the measure of \(\angle CJK\), written \(m\angle LVM=m\angle CJK\), then \(\angle LVM\) is congruent to \(\angle CJK\), written \(\angle LVM\ \cong \angle CJK\). Note: With an equal sign, the \(m\) is used to show that the measurements are equal but with a congruent symbol, the \(m\) is NOT used because congruence compares figures and not the numbers.

Relationships of Lines

There are three different types of line relationships. Review the following and pay close attention to labels both in writing and in diagrams.

1. Parallel Lines

Parallel lines have two conditions that must be true:

  1. They never intersect, meaning that they do not cross.
  2. They lie on the same plane or flat surface.

Here, \(\overleftrightarrow {AB}\) is parallel to \(\overleftrightarrow {DC}\) on plane \(Q\). Notice the arrowheads between the points on each line; an equal number of arrowheads means the lines are parallel. The symbol for parallel is \(\parallel\). The shorthand way to write \(\overleftrightarrow {AB}\) is parallel to \(\overleftrightarrow {DC}\) is \(\overleftrightarrow {AB}\parallel \overleftrightarrow {DC}\).

2. Perpendicular Lines

Perpendicular Lines are lines that intersect right angles \((90^{\circ})\). Lines MUST create \(90^{\circ}\) angles in order to be perpendicular. A box shows that a right angle is created. Since the lines must intersect, the lines will always lie on the same plane.

Here, \(\overleftrightarrow {UN}\) is perpendicular to \(\overleftrightarrow {LV}\) on plane \(R\). The box at the intersection of the two lines shows that they intersect at right angles.

The symbol for perpendicular in print is \(\perp\). Therefore, the shorthand way to write \(\overleftrightarrow {UN}\) is perpendicular to \(\overleftrightarrow {LV}\) is \(\overleftrightarrow {UN}\perp \overleftrightarrow {LV}\).

3. Skew Lines

Skew lines have two conditions that must be true:

  1. They never intersect.
  2. They lie on different planes.

Here, \(\overleftrightarrow {DC}\) is skew to \(\overleftrightarrow {FG}\) because they will never intersect, even when extended, and they are on different planes. Use arrows on the line segments because this shows that the full lines compare to each other.

There is no symbol for skew. Other examples of skew are lines \(\overleftrightarrow {AE}\) with \(\overleftrightarrow {BC}\) or \(\overleftrightarrow {CG}\) with \(\overleftrightarrow {AD}\).

Definition

Polygons are two-dimensional figures (that lie on one plane) and have the following three requirements:

  1. Only segments for sides (no curves)
  2. Closed (no openings)
  3. No crossing segments (sides do not pass through each other)

Note that since polygons have no curves, a circle is never a polygon.

Classification of Polygons by Number of Sides

Polygons are classified (or named) by the number of sides or angles they have. The number of sides is used as the prefix. See below for all of the names used in the course. For names that you are not familiar with, it is recommended that you create vocabulary flashcards to review. If using traditional notecards, write the name of the polygon on the front and the number of sides and a diagram on the back.

Classification of Polygons by Type

Concave Polygon

A concave polygon is defined as a polygon with one or more interior (inside) angles greater than \(180^{\circ}\). When an interior angle is greater than \(180^{\circ}\), that vertex will point inward and look like it has â€œcavedâ€ into the polygon.

Convex Polygon

A convex polygon is defined as a polygon with all interior angles less than \(180^{\circ}\). All vertices of the polygon will point outwards, away from the interior of the shape. A polygon can never be both concave and convex at the same time because they are opposites.

Above, the concave polygon has two sides that collapse into the interior of the figure. Since the angle created inside the figure is greater than \(180^{\circ}\), the figure is concave. The convex polygon has all of its angles point away from the interior of the figure so it is not concave.

Unless stated in words or in given labels, figures can not be assumed to have congruent sides or angles.
