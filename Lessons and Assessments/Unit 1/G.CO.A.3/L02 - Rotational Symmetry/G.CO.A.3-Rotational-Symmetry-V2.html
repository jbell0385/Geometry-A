1. What is Rotational Symmetry?
Instruction Examples Video Self-Check

Definition

A complete rotation is \(360^{\circ}\). A figure has rotational symmetry when it can be rotated less than \(360^{\circ}\) about a point, called the center of rotation, and appear to look the same.

To decide the degree of rotation, divide \(360^{\circ}\) by the number of times the figure can be turned while it still looks identical to the original before it reaches the original position again.

180째 Rotational Symmetry

The figure to the right has a rotational symmetry of \(180^{\circ}\). To easily identify \(180^{\circ}\) rotational symmetry, when turned upside down, the figure will look like the original.

During rotation, a figure has point symmetry if every matching part is the same distance from a center point but faces the opposite direction. Therefore, when a figure has \(180^{\circ}\) rotational symmetry, it has point symmetry.

Different Degrees of Rotation

The following have various degrees of rotation, from \(0^{\circ}\) to \(90^{\circ}\).

  • Determine the degrees of rotation.
    • Count the number of times each figure can be turned until it lands or maps back on top of its original position.
    • 360 divided by this number will give you the degree.
  • Then determine the number of times (called distinct degrees of rotation) the figure can be rotated before it reaches \(360^{\circ}\).

To get started, remember to count the number of times each figure can be turned until it lands or maps back on top of its original position. \(360^{\circ}\) divided by this number will give you the degree.

2. Rotational Symmetry in Regular Polygons and Circles
Instruction Examples Video Self-Check

Regular polygons have the following characteristics:

  • Closed, noncurved figures
  • All sides are congruent
  • All angles are congruent

The first degree of rotational symmetry of a regular polygon is found by taking 360 (a full rotation) and dividing it by the number of sides (n) of the polygon.

A regular heptagon (seen at right) has seven sides. \(\frac {360^{\circ }} {7}\approx 51.4^{\circ }\) for each turn. Each distinct degree of rotation can be found by adding an extra \(51.4^{\circ}\).

Circles are figures in which every point on the circle is the same distance (also called equidistant) from a center point. Since all points on a circle can be rotated on top of all other points, a circle has an infinite number of degrees of rotation.

Rotational Symmetry in Regular Polygons

List all of the distinct degrees of rotational symmetry for each of the regular polygons below.

Example 2.1: Regular Triangle

A triangle has rotational symmetry every \(120^{\circ}\) because:

  • A triangle has three sides.
  • Divide 360 by the number of sides (3). (\(\frac {360^{\circ}} {3}=120^{\circ}\)).
  • Therefore, the distinct degrees of rotation before the final \(360^{\circ}\) are:
    • \(120^{\circ}\)
    • \(240^{\circ}\) (\(120^{\circ}+120^{\circ}=240^{\circ}\))

Example 2.2: Regular Quadrilateral or Square

A regular quadrilateral or square has rotational symmetry every \(90^{\circ}\) because:

  • Squares have four sides.
  • Divide 360 by the number of sides (4). \(\frac {360^{\circ}} {4}=90^{\circ}\).
  • Therefore, the distinct degrees of rotation before the final \(360^{\circ}\) are:
    • \(90^{\circ}\)
    • \(180^{\circ}\) (\(90^{\circ}+90^{\circ}=180^{\circ}\))
    • \(270^{\circ}\) \((90^{\circ}+90^{\circ}+90^{\circ}=270^{\circ}\))
