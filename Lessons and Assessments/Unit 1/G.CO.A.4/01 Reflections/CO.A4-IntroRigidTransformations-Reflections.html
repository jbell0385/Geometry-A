1. Rigid Transformations
Instruction Examples Interactive Self-Check

Introduction to Transformations

  • A transformation is any change to a figure on a plane.
  • A transformation that preserves the shape and size is called a rigid transformation.
  • There are three types of rigid transformations:
    • Reflections
    • Translations
    • Rotations

Watch the video at the right for an introduction to the three types of transformations.

Definition of Rigid Transformations

Rigid transformations are transformations that:

  • Do not affect size
  • Do not affect shape
  • Do not affect angle measure
  • Have congruent pre-images and images

Naming Conventions

When naming a pre-image and image, it is important to name the points in a specific way so it is clear which is which.

The pre-image is written by naming its points or vertices. The image will be written with a prime symbol, which is an apostrophe. In the example to the right, the pre-image triangle is named \(ABC\) while the image is named \(A’B’C’\) (which is said as A prime, B prime, C prime).

In this example:

  1. \(A\) corresponds with \(A’\)
  2. \(B\) corresponds with \(B’\)
  3. \(C\) corresponds with \(C’\)

2. Attributes of Reflection
Instruction Examples Video Self-Check

Reflection Appearance

A reflection is the folding or flipping of a figure over a line of reflection.

A pre-image and its reflection image:

  • are congruent.
  • have the same size.
  • have the same shape.
  • face opposite directions.
  • are mirror images of each other.

Reflection Distance

Each point on a reflected image is the same distance from the line of reflection as the original point.

Reflection Notation

Here is an example of reflection notation:
\[r_{(x=2)}\triangle ABC\]

  1. The \(r\) stands for rotation.
  2. The \(x=2\) represents the line of reflection.
  3. The \(\triangle ABC\) is what you are reflecting.

3. Create Reflections
Instruction Examples Video Self-Check

Step 1: Graph the Line of Reflection

If the line of reflection is only given but not graphed, you must graph it first.

Step 2: Find the Distance from Each Point to the Line

Find the distance and direction between each point and the line of reflection of the pre-image.

Step 3: Create the Image

Find the same distance and direction on the opposite side of the line of reflection. Mark the new points using the prime symbol. Do this for each point of the pre-image. Lastly, connect the points to create the reflected triangle.

Step 4: Check Your Work

Check your distances and direction. The image to the line of reflection is always opposite of the direction of the pre-image.

Create a Reflection

Example 3.1

Reflect quadrilateral \(FGHK\) over \(x = 4\). Find the coordinates of \(G’\).

Solution

Follow the four steps to reflect in order to obtain \(G’(1,6)\).

Example 3.2

Reflect rectangle \(KLMN\) over \(y = x\) (This line is created where every \(y\) value is equal to every \(x\) value such as at points \((1,1)\), \((2,2)\) etc.). After the reflection, give the coordinates of \(N’\).

Solution

The rule to reflect over the line \(y = x\) is to reverse the ordered pair. For example, here the reflection over \(y = x\) for \(N(-4,1)\) is \(N’(1,-4)\).
