Conditional Statements
×

Conditionals Statements are sentences using logical reasoning that use the format if followed by then. The if portion at the beginning of the statement sets the requirement or condition called the hypothesis for the situation and the then portion is the result called the conclusion of the sentence. The hypothesis is assumed true unless otherwise stated.

Example: If it is raining, then I will get wet.

Here, the condition or hypothesis is ‘it is raining’ and the result or conclusion is ‘I will get wet’. We assume it is raining in order for the second half of the sentence to be true.

Types of Angles:

Transversal Angles
×

Transversal Angles:

  1. Alternate Interior Angles: Angles inbetween the lines and on opposite sides of the transversal.
  2. Alternate Exterior Angles: Angles outside the lines and on opposite sides of the transversal.
  3. Same-Side Interior Angles: Angles inbetween the lines and on the same side of the transversal.
  4. Corresponding Angles: Angles on the same side of the transversal and in the same relative position.

Two-Column Proof

A two-column proof is one way to create a clearly organized process in order to show that a conjecture, or educated guess, is true. It consists of a list of statements written in the left column, and the reasons for why each of the statements can be made written in the right column. Every step of the proof (that is, each conclusion that is made) is a row in the two-column proof. The following is an example of an algebraic proof.

Algebraic Properties of Equality

Review the following algebraic properties of equality. These will be used in the following algebraic proofs. The last three properties on the list are new to you. Please be sure to create vocabulary flash cards for any words that you have forgotten or do not know.

Algebraic Properties of Equality
Addition Property of Equality If \(a=b\), then \(a + c = b + c\)
Subtraction Property of Equality If \(a=b\), then \(a-c=b-c\)
Multiplication Property of Equality If \(a=b\), then \(a\cdot c=b\cdot c\)
Division Property of Equality If \(a=b\), then \(\frac{a}{c}=\frac{b}{c}\)
Combine Like Terms \(5x + 2x = 14\)
\(7x\)\(= 14\)
Distributive Property of Multiplication Over Addition or Subtraction. \(a\left(b+c\right)=ab+ac\) \(a\left(b-c\right)=ab-ac\)
Substitution Property of Equality If \(a=b\), then in any expression \(a\) could be substituted with \(b\).
Reflexive Property of Equality For any real number \(a, a=a\).
Symmetric Property of Equality If \(a=b\), then \(b=a\).
Transitive Property of Equality If \(a=b\) and \(b=c\), then \(a=c\).

Steps to help Write a Two-Column Proof

The following steps will help guide you through proofs for the rest of the course. Two-column proofs are helpful because they break complicated topics into individual steps.They allow you to develop critical analysis skills and enable you to defend your answers more clearly.

Step 1: Read the problem over carefully. You can start the proof with all of the given information or add them in as they make sense within the proof. The prove statement is the final step, or answer, to your proof.

Step 2: If necessary, draw a picture of the problem to help you visualize what is given and what you want to prove. This is especially helpful for geometric proofs. Include labels that will help you see congruent angles, congruent segments, parallel lines, or other important details if necessary.

Step 3: Use the prove statement to guide your statements in the proof. Each should get closer to proving the final statement true and must have a reason which includes definitions, postulates, or theorems for justification.

Step 4: Use the statement from each row to help you create the next statement. Steps must follow a logical order and are built off of the prior step.

Step 5: Once you have arrived at your solution, read through the two-column proof you’ve written to be assured that each step has the correct reasoning. This helps emphasize the clarity and effectiveness of your argument.

3. Prove Lines Parallel
Instruction Examples Video Self-Check

Certain angle pairs can be used to show that two lines are parallel. Previously, you learned that if two or more lines are parallel and have a transversal, special angle pairs must be either congruent or supplementary. Now you will learn that the converse is also true, which changes the order of a statement and says it backwards. Therefore, the converse states that if certain angle pairs are congruent or supplementary, then the lines must be parallel.

Converse of the Corresponding Angles Theorem

If two lines are cut by a transversal so that corresponding angles are congruent, then the lines must be parallel.

Converse of the Alternate Exterior Angles Theorem

If two lines are cut by a transversal so that alternate exterior angles are congruent, then the lines must be parallel.

Converse of the Alternate Interior Angles Theorem

If two lines are cut by a transversal so that alternate interior angles are congruent, then the lines must be parallel.

Converse to the Same Side Interior Angles Theorem

If two lines are cut by a transversal so that same side interior angles are supplementary, then the lines must be parallel.

Transitive Property of Parallel Lines

The transitive property can also be applied to parallel lines:

  • If two lines are parallel to the same line, then they are also parallel to each other.
  • If \(p\parallel q\) and \(q\parallel r\) then \(p\parallel r\).

Example 3.1

Decide which lines are parallel. Not all lines must be parallel in a diagram when a pair of angles are congruent. You must only choose the two lines that share the same transversal which the angles touch.

Use the figure and the given information to determine which lines, if any, are parallel. Justify your answer using one of the theorems.

  1. If \(\angle 9\cong \angle 16\), then _____\(\parallel \)_____ by the _____________________________________
  2. If \(\angle 5\cong \angle 13\), then _____\(\parallel \)_____ by the _____________________________________
  3. If \(\angle 1\cong \angle 6\), then _____\(\parallel \)_____ by the _____________________________________

Solution:
  1. If \(\angle 9\cong \angle 16\), then \(a\parallel b\) by the converse of the alternate exterior angles theorem.
  2. If \(\angle 5\cong \angle 13\), then \(c\parallel d\) by the converse of the corresponding angles theorem.
  3. If \(\angle 1\cong \angle 6\), then no lines must be parallel because \(\angle 1\) and \( \angle 6\) are vertical angles which do not use parallel lines when congruent. More information is needed.
