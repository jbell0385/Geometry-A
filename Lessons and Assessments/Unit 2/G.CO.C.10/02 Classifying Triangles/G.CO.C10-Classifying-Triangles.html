Congruence
×

Congruence is when two figures have the same shape and size.

  1. Congruent angles are angles that have the same degree measure.
  2. Congruent segments have the same length.
  3. Congruent shapes have both congruent angles and congruent segments.

Types of Angles:

  1. Right Angle: An angle that is \(90^{\circ}\).
  2. Obtuse: An angle that is greater than \(90^{\circ}\).
  3. Acute Angle: An angle that is less than \(90^{\circ}\).

Classify Triangles by Their Angles

The following table classifies triangles by the relationships that their angles have. Be sure to create flash cards for any types of triangles for which you are not familiar.

Name Definition Image
Acute Triangle A triangle with all three angles less than \(90^{\circ }\) (acute).
Right Triangle A triangle with only one right angle (exactly \(90^{\circ }\)).
Obtuse Triangle A triangle with only one angle greater than \(90^{\circ }\) (obtuse) and two acute angles.
Equiangular A triangle with three congruent interior angles. Because the interior angles of any triangle always add up to \(180^{\circ }\), each angle is always that value divided by three, or \(60^{\circ }\).

Classify Triangles by Their Side Lengths

The following table classifies triangles by the relationships that their sides have. Be sure to create flash cards for any types of triangles or their parts for which you are not familiar.

Name Definition Image
Scalene Triangle A triangle that has three noncongruent sides. In other words, the sides have different lengths. If no dash marks are used on the sides, the triangle is understood to be scalene. Since all sides are different, all angle measures must also be different.
Isosceles Triangle A triangle that has two OR MORE congruent sides.
Important parts include:
  • Legs: the two congruent sides;
  • Base, the noncongruent side;
  • Vertex angle: the intersection where the two congruent sides meet;
  • Base angles: the two congruent angles opposite (across from) the legs.
Equilateral Triangle A triangle that has all three congruent sides. Since equilateral triangles have more than two equal sides, they are also must be isosceles. Since they have three congruent sides, they must be equiangular as well.

Classify Triangles by its Angles and Sides

A triangle can have more than one classification and therefore could have its own unique special name. For example, examine an “isosceles right triangle.” An isosceles triangle has two sides that are congruent and a right triangle will have one 90° angle. Therefore, an isosceles right triangle would have both properties.

Example 1.1

Can a triangle have more than 1 obtuse angle?

Solution:

No, an obtuse angle is defined to be more than \(90^{\circ }\) and all angles in a triangle add up to \(180^{\circ }\); therefore it would be impossible for a triangle to have two angles that are more than \(90^{\circ }\) (or obtuse) each because the sum would be greater than \(180^{\circ }\).

Equilateral and Equiangular

Equilateral Triangle Theorem

If a triangle is equilateral, then the triangle is equiangular.

Equiangular Triangle Theorem

If a triangle is equiangular, then the triangle is equilateral.
Isosceles Triangle

Isosceles Triangle Theorem:

If two sides (the legs) of a triangle are congruent, then the angles opposite those sides (the base angles) are congruent. If \(\overline{AB}\cong \overline{CB}\), then \(\angle A\cong \angle C\).

Converse of Isosceles Triangle Theorem:

If two base angles of a triangle are congruent, then the legs opposite those angles are congruent. If \(\angle A\cong \angle C\), then \(\overline{AB}\cong \overline{CB}\).

Line of Symmetry In Isosceles Triangles

An isosceles triangle has reflectional symmetry by the angle bisector through its vertex angle. If line \(\overline{BD}\) is the line of symmetry for isosceles \(\triangle ABC\) then, \(\angle ABD\cong \angle CBD\) and \(\overline{AD}\cong \overline{CD}\).
