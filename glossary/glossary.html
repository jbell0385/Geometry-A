Naming Lines
×

Lines can be named using the following two methods:

  1. Use any two points on the line with a segment above the letters. Arrows must be on both sides of the segment. Points are interchangeable but you must use exactly two. Here, some possible names are \(\overleftrightarrow {HA}, \overleftrightarrow {AH}, \overleftrightarrow {TA}, \overleftrightarrow {TH}\), etc.
  2. Use the lower case cursive letter. This is not a point and can not be combined with any other letters. Here, the final possible name is line m.

Dilation
×

A dilation is an image of a figure or object that has had a change in size because all of its lengths have been multiplied by a scale factor. If the figure increases in size when dilated, it is called an enlargement. If the figure decreases in size when dilated, it is called a reduction. A dilation is NOT a rigid transformation because it does not retain its original size.

Close
Scale Factor
×

Scale Factor is a value either less than or greater than one that is multiplied to all lengths of a figure or object in order to create a dilation.

Types of Triangles
×

Classification by Angle Measures

Classification Definition Diagram
Acute Acute Triangles have all three angles with measures less than \(90^{\circ }\).
Right Right Triangles have only one angle equal to \(90^{\circ }\) and the other two angles less than \(90^{\circ }\).
Eqiangular Acute Triangles have all three angles congruent to each other. Since the sum of angle measures is \(180^{\circ }\), each angle can be found by dividing the sum by three, so the measure of each is \(60^{\circ }\). Equiangular triangles must also be equilateral.

Classification by Side Lengths:

Classification Definition Diagram
Scalene Scalene Triangles have NO sides congruent. All three must have different lengths.
Isosceles Isosceles Triangles have AT LEAST two sides congruent. This means that they can have either two or three sides with equal lengths.
Equilateral Equilateral Triangles must have ALL three sides congruent. Equilateral triangles must also be equiangular.

Absolute Value
×

Absolute Value is defined as the distance a number is away from zero on a number line. Since the distance between two points must be positive, the absolute value of a number is always positive. The symbol for absolute value is two vertical lines surrounding the number.

Examples:

  • \(\left|7.2\right| = 7.2\) because the number is \(7.2\) units away from zero.
  • \(\left|-329\right| = 329\) because the number is \(329\) units away from zero.

Conditional Statements
×

Conditionals Statements are sentences using logical reasoning that use the format if followed by then. The if portion at the beginning of the statement sets the requirement or condition called the hypothesis for the situation and the then portion is the result called the conclusion of the sentence. The hypothesis is assumed true unless otherwise stated.

Example: If it is raining, then I will get wet.

Here, the condition or hypothesis is ‘it is raining’ and the result or conclusion is ‘I will get wet’. We assume it is raining in order for the second half of the sentence to be true.

Fractions
×

Adding Fractions

To add fractions together do the following:

  1. Find the common denominator (bottom number).
  2. Multiply each fraction by the other fraction's denominator
  3. Add the numerators (top number)

Subtracting Fractions

To add fractions together do the following:

  1. Find the common denominator (bottom number).
  2. Multiply each fraction by the other fraction's denominator
  3. Subtract the numerators (top number)

Multiplying Fractions

To add fractions together do the following:

  1. Multiply the numerators (top number) together.
  2. Multiply the denominators (bottom number) together.

Dividing Fractions

To add fractions together do the following:

  1. Use the reciprocal (flip upside down) of the second fraction
  2. Multiply the numerators
  3. Multiply the denominators

AA Similarity Postulate
×

Angle-Angle (AA) Similarity Postulate has the following:

  1. The two triangles must have two pairs of congruent corresponding angles.
  2. \(\angle A\cong \angle A'\)
  3. \(\angle B\cong \angle B'\)
  4. \(\triangle ABC\sim \triangle A'B'C'\)
Close
SAS Similarity Postulate
×

Side-Angle-Side (SAS) Similarity Postulate has the following:

  1. The two triangles must have two pairs of corresponding sides with the same ratio.
    • \(\frac{6}{3}=2\)
    • \(\frac{8}{4}=2\)
  2. The corresponding included angles must be congruent.
Close
SSS Similarity Postulate
×

Side-Side-Side (SSS) Similarity Postulate has the following:

  1. The two triangles must have three pairs of corresponding sides with the same ratio.
  2. \(\frac{A}{A'}=\frac{B}{B'}=\frac{C}{C'}\)

Congruence is when two figures have the same shape and size.

  2. Congruent segments have the same length.
  3. Congruent shapes have both congruent angles and congruent segments.

×

When you have two lines crossed by a transversal, Alternate Interior Angles (AIA) are angles on the inner side of two lines, but on opposite sides of the transversal.

When the lines are parallel, then the alternate interior angles are congruent.

Alternate Exterior Angles
×

When you have two lines crossed by a transversal, Alternate Exterior Angles (AEA) are angles on the outer side of the two lines, but on opposite sides of the transversal.

When the lines are parallel, then the alternate exterior angles are congruent.

Same-Side Interior Angles
×

When you have two lines crossed by a transversal, Same-Side Interior Angles (SSIA) are angles on the inner side of the two lines, and on the same side of the transversal.

When the lines are parallel, then the sum of the Same-Side Interior Angles equals \(180^{\circ}\).

×

Two-Column Proofs
×

A two-column proof is a structure for creating geometric proofs. A two column table is created with statements on the left side and reasons on the right side.

Two-Column Proofs Example

Given: \(line\ a\parallel line\ b\)
\(\angle 1 = 150^{\circ}\)

Prove: \(\angle 6 30^{\circ}\)

  1. Line \(a\) is parallel to line \(b\). That is given.
  2. Angle 1 equals \(150^{\circ}\)
  3. Since we know that the lines are parallel, then we can use same-side interior angles to determine that angle 6 is \(30^{\circ}\)

SSS Congruence Postulate
×

The Side-Side-Side (SSS) Congruence Postulate states that if two triangles have 3 pairs of corresponding sides that are congruent, then the triangles are congruent.

Close
SAS Congruence Postulate
×

The Side-Angle-Side (SAS) Congruence Postulate states that if two triangles have 2 pairs of corresponding sides that are congruent and the including angles are congruent, then the triangles are congruent.

Close
ASA Congruence Postulate
×

The Angle-Side-Angle (ASA) Congruence Postulate states that if two triangles have 1 pair of corresponding sides that are congruent and 2 pairs of congruent angles that include the congruent sides, then the triangles are congruent.

Close
AAS Congruence Postulate
×

The Angle-Angle-Side (AAS) Congruence Postulate states that if two triangles have 2 pairs of congruent angles and 1 non-included congruent sides, then the triangles are congruent.

Close
HL Congruence Postulate
×

The Hypotenuse-Leg (HL) Congruence Postulate states that if two triangles are right triangles, the hypotenuses are congruent, and one other corresponding leg pair is congruent, then the triangles are congruent.
